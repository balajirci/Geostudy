Competition Format — What to Expect at Nationals
Key fact: The 2025 National Final at 50 Sussex Drive (Ottawa) brought together 20 finalists aged 12–16 from every region of Canada for three days of written tests, outdoor fieldwork, and live-audience challenges covering both local and global geography.

📝 Part 1: Written Test

  • Short-answer and essay questions
  • Covers two specific geographic subjects (revealed in advance)
  • No multiple choice — detailed written responses required
  • 2 hours to complete
  • Top scorers advance to fieldwork

🧭 Part 2: Fieldwork Exercise

  • Outdoor, hands-on geographic observation
  • Map reading and sketch mapping
  • Environmental analysis and data collection
  • Usually conducted in the Ottawa region
  • Top 5 fieldwork scores advance to live final

🎙️ Part 3: Live Final (Game Show Format)

  • Top 5 compete; scores reset to zero
  • Rapid-fire trivia, live audience
  • Both English and French permitted
  • Human and physical geography at local and global scales
  • Biodiversity, world places, exploration themes
Strategy tip: Your written test and fieldwork scores determine if you reach the live final — but they don't carry over. The live final is a completely fresh start. Master both depth (written) AND speed (trivia).

Physical Geography of Canada
Why this matters: The multimedia test and written components explicitly cover landforms, climate, and resource development. Expect map identification, structural geology questions, and physiographic region analysis.
Canada's 7 Physiographic Regions — Master These

1. Canadian Shield

Canada's largest and oldest landform region — covers roughly half the country. Centred on Hudson Bay. Composed of exposed Precambrian crystalline igneous and metamorphic rocks, over 540 million years old. Very thin, nutrient-poor soil. Rich in minerals: nickel, gold, uranium, diamonds. Contains thousands of lakes carved by glaciation. Surrounds Hudson Bay and extends through Ontario, Quebec, Nunavut, Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Alberta, and the NWT.

2. Cordillera (Western Mountains)

A series of mountain belts separated by plateaus and valleys, approximately 680 km wide. Includes the Rocky Mountains, Coast Mountains, Columbia Mountains, and Mackenzie Mountains. Formed by tectonic collision — the Pacific Plate forcing under the North American Plate. Contains Canada's highest peak: Mount Logan (5,959 m) in Yukon, the highest point in Canada and second-highest in North America. Part of the Pacific Ring of Fire — active volcanic and earthquake zone. Spans BC, Yukon, and parts of Alberta and NWT.

3. Interior Plains (Prairies)

Covers most of Alberta, Saskatchewan, and southern Manitoba, plus parts of NWT. Known as Canada's "breadbasket" — major wheat and canola growing region. Rich sedimentary soils from ancient shallow seas and glacial deposits. Three prairie levels rise westward: Manitoba lowlands → Saskatchewan plain → Alberta foothills. Rich in petroleum and natural gas (especially Alberta). Part of the Great Plains of North America.

4. Great Lakes–St. Lawrence Lowlands

The smallest physiographic region but the most populated — home to nearly one-third of all Canadians. Covers southern Ontario and southern Quebec. Includes Toronto, Montreal, Ottawa, Hamilton, and Windsor. Flat to gently rolling terrain from glacial deposits. The St. Lawrence Lowland is a rift valley formed by faulting. Major agricultural zone (tender fruit in Niagara, dairy farming). Economic heartland of Canada.

5. Appalachian Region

Old, eroded mountain range extending through New Brunswick, Nova Scotia, PEI, Newfoundland, and the Gaspé Peninsula of Quebec. Approximately 380 million years old — among North America's oldest exposed mountains. Rolling hills with river valleys. Chic-Choc, Notre-Dame, and Long Range Mountains are key ranges. Atlantic fisheries historically centred here. Includes the Bay of Fundy with the world's highest tides.

6. Hudson Bay and Arctic Lowlands

Low, flat, poorly drained land surrounding Hudson Bay and James Bay, plus the High Arctic islands. Covered in muskeg (waterlogged boggy terrain). Ground remains frozen much of the year (permafrost). Sparse population. Contains significant oil, gas, and lignite (coal) deposits in Arctic sedimentary rock layers.

7. Arctic Lands (Innuitian Mountains + Arctic Archipelago)

Canada's most northerly region — the Queen Elizabeth Islands and surrounding waters. Innuitian Mountains peak above 2,500 m. Largely unexplored and very sparsely populated. Permanent ice caps on Ellesmere Island. Home to polar bears, muskoxen, Arctic foxes. Increasingly significant for resource extraction and the Northwest Passage shipping route.

Great Lakes — Key Facts

  • Superior — largest by surface area (world's largest freshwater lake by area); shared with USA
  • Huron — second-largest Great Lake
  • Erie — shallowest; most polluted historically; Niagara Falls at its outflow
  • Ontario — smallest of the Great Lakes by surface area; drains into St. Lawrence River
  • The Great Lakes contain 21% of the world's surface fresh water
  • Together they form the largest freshwater system on Earth

Other Major Lakes

  • Great Bear Lake (NWT) — largest lake entirely within Canada
  • Great Slave Lake (NWT) — deepest lake in North America (614 m); Mackenzie River begins here
  • Lake Winnipeg (MB) — 13th-largest lake in the world; massive but shallow
  • Lake Athabasca (AB/SK) — near the Athabasca oil sands
Glaciation — Canada's Most Powerful Geographic Force

During the last Ice Age (ending roughly 10,000 years ago), massive ice sheets up to 3 km thick covered virtually all of Canada. The effects are still visible everywhere:

  • Drumlins — elongated hills formed under glaciers; abundant in Ontario
  • Eskers — long winding ridges of sand and gravel; common in Canadian Shield
  • Kettles — depressions formed when ice blocks melted; often filled with water (prairie potholes)
  • Moraines — ridges of glacial debris; the Oak Ridges Moraine north of Toronto is a key example
  • Fjords — deep, narrow coastal inlets carved by glaciers; abundant in BC and Labrador
  • Canadian Shield thin soils — glaciers scraped away soil, exposing bedrock
  • Prairie fertile soils — glacial deposits created deep, rich soils for agriculture
  • Thousands of lakes — glacial scouring + meltwater filled depressions; Canada has ~2 million lakes

Climate Zones, Weather Patterns & Climate Change

Continental Climate

Interior and Prairie provinces. Extreme temperature ranges — bitterly cold winters (can drop below -40°C), warm summers. Low precipitation. Dry, open landscape.

Key cities: Winnipeg, Regina, Saskatoon

Oceanic (Maritime West)

BC coast. Mild, rainy winters; warm, drier summers. Temperate rainforest conditions. Vancouver Island shields coast from major storms.

Key cities: Vancouver, Victoria, Prince Rupert

Maritime East

Atlantic provinces. Influenced by cold Labrador Current. More precipitation, fog, and storms than the west coast. Cooler summers.

Key cities: Halifax, St. John's, Moncton

Humid Continental

Southern Ontario and Quebec. Four distinct seasons. Cold winters, warm-to-hot summers. Moderate precipitation year-round. Snow cover 3–5 months.

Key cities: Toronto, Montreal, Ottawa

Subarctic

Boreal forest zone across most of northern Canada. Very long, cold winters; short, cool summers. Permafrost in many areas. Low precipitation but high snowfall.

Arctic / Tundra

Canada's far north. Permanent frozen ground (permafrost). Very low precipitation (polar desert conditions in some areas). Temperatures rarely rise above 10°C even in summer. 24-hour daylight in summer, polar night in winter.

Climate Change in Canada — A National Priorities Issue

Canada is warming at approximately twice the global average rate — the Arctic is warming at roughly three times the global average. This is a major focus of Canadian Geographic magazine and a near-certain topic at the national finals.

Key Climate Change Facts for Canada

  • Arctic summer sea ice has been declining sharply — multi-year ice (older, thicker) is being replaced by thin seasonal ice
  • Permafrost thaw is destabilizing infrastructure in northern communities — roads, buildings, runways
  • The Northwest Passage is increasingly navigable as sea ice retreats, with major implications for shipping and sovereignty
  • Forest fires have increased dramatically in size and frequency across the boreal forest — 2023 was a record wildfire year in Canada
  • Rising sea levels threaten low-lying coastal areas, particularly Atlantic communities and the Fraser River delta
  • Glacier retreat in the Rockies and BC Coast Range threatens freshwater supplies downstream
  • Species range shifts: many plants and animals moving northward or to higher elevations
  • Boil water advisories affect dozens of Indigenous communities — a major equity and human geography issue

Inuit Observations (from Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada)

Inuit communities in the Arctic and Subarctic have documented unprecedented sea ice loss, ocean acidification, permafrost melt, and coastal erosion. Climate change undermines access to country foods (wild-harvested foods), increases on-ice hazards, and threatens cultural sovereignty. Inuit Nunangat (the Inuit homeland across the Arctic) is at the forefront of climate impacts.

Iconic Canadian Wildlife — Know These for Trivia
  • Polar bear — "sea bear"; Canada has 60% of the world's polar bears; Churchill, MB is the "Polar Bear Capital of the World"; threatened by sea ice loss
  • Woodland caribou — national icon; found in boreal forest; threatened by habitat fragmentation from resource extraction
  • Spirit bear (Kermode bear) — white-furred black bear; found only in the Great Bear Rainforest, BC; sacred to First Nations; "ghost bear"
  • Beaver — Canada's national animal; dam-builder; critical ecosystem engineer (creates wetlands); featured on the nickel
  • Pronghorn — fastest land animal in North America; found in southern prairies; threatened by fencing; the subject of a recent Can Geo story (2025)
  • Atlantic salmon — iconic but severely endangered; subject of conservation battles in NB and NS
  • Whooping crane — Canada's tallest bird; one of North America's most endangered species; breeds at Wood Buffalo NP
  • Lake sturgeon — ancient fish; some individuals over 100 years old; threatened by habitat loss and overfishing
  • Right whale (North Atlantic) — critically endangered; accidentally killed in fishing gear and by ship strikes in Gulf of St. Lawrence

Indigenous Peoples — A Core Theme at Nationals
Critical for finals: The Canadian Geographic Challenge and Can Geo magazine both place major emphasis on Indigenous geography, knowledge systems, and current issues. This is virtually guaranteed to appear in your written test and live trivia. The Indigenous Peoples Atlas of Canada is the primary RCGS reference — study it.
The Three Groups: First Nations, Inuit, and Métis

First Nations

More than 630 distinct First Nations in Canada, representing over 50 language families and hundreds of dialects. Geographic distribution spans all regions: from Coast Salish peoples on BC's coast, to Haudenosaunee (Six Nations) in Ontario, to Cree nations across the boreal forest, to Mi'kmaq in Atlantic Canada. Traditional territories do not align with provincial boundaries.

  • Coast Salish, Haida, Tlingit, Tsimshian — Pacific Northwest coast; totem poles; salmon cultures
  • Nehiyawak (Cree) — largest First Nation by population; spans from Quebec to Alberta; boreal forest and plains Cree
  • Anishinaabe (Ojibwe/Chippewa) — Great Lakes region; birchbark canoes; Algonquian language family
  • Haudenosaunee (Iroquois Confederacy) — Six Nations: Mohawk, Oneida, Onondaga, Cayuga, Seneca, Tuscarora; longhouse culture; Wampum records
  • Dene Nation — NWT and northern BC; Athabascan language speakers
  • Blackfoot Confederacy — southern Alberta plains; treaty relationships with Crown

Inuit

Approximately 65,000 Inuit in Canada. Their traditional homeland is Inuit Nunangat, which covers four regions:

  • Nunatsiavut — northern Labrador
  • Nunavik — northern Quebec
  • Nunavut — territory created in 1999 (Inuit-majority government)
  • Inuvialuit Settlement Region — NWT and Yukon Arctic coast

Inuktitut is the primary Inuit language. Traditional knowledge (Inuit Qaujimajatuqangit) is integral to understanding Arctic environments. Key food sources: caribou, seal, beluga whale, Arctic char.

Métis

A distinct Indigenous people of mixed First Nations and European (primarily French) ancestry. Historically centred in the Red River Settlement (Winnipeg area). Louis Riel led the Red River Resistance (1869–70) and Northwest Resistance (1885). The Métis National Council represents Métis across western Canada. Distinct culture: Michif language (blend of Cree and French), Red River jig, beaded floral art.

Current Issues — What Can Geo Magazine Covers

Boil Water Advisories

Many First Nations communities have faced long-term boil water advisories — some lasting over 25 years. Neskantaga First Nation in northern Ontario experienced a 30-year boil water advisory, the longest in Canadian history (covered in Can Geo January/February 2025). The federal government committed to ending all long-term advisories, but progress has been uneven.

Land Rights and Sovereignty

Treaties between Indigenous nations and the Crown govern much of Canada's land. Many treaty agreements are disputed or were never honoured. Comprehensive land claims (modern treaties) continue to be negotiated, particularly in BC where many treaties were never signed.

Residential Schools and Truth & Reconciliation

Over 150,000 Indigenous children were forced into residential schools over more than a century. The Truth and Reconciliation Commission's 94 Calls to Action (2015) guide reconciliation work. In 2021, discoveries of unmarked graves at former school sites shook Canada nationally. Orange Shirt Day (September 30) is now a federal statutory holiday — National Day for Truth and Reconciliation.

Indigenous-Led Conservation

Many Indigenous nations are leading conservation initiatives using Traditional Ecological Knowledge (TEK) alongside Western science. Examples include:

  • Guardian programs — Indigenous stewards protecting traditional territories
  • Blueberry River First Nations' successful legal case limiting industrial activity in their territory (BC, 2021)
  • Great Bear Rainforest agreements involving Coastal First Nations in BC
  • Can Geo (Jan/Feb 2025): "Tyonnhehkwen: The Life Sustainers" — weaving Indigenous and settler knowledge for sustainable land use

Human Geography — Population, Cities & Culture
Canadian Population Geography

Canada is the world's second-largest country by land area (~9.98 million km²) but with a population of approximately 40 million (2024). This makes it one of the most sparsely populated large nations on Earth.

Settlement Patterns

  • Roughly 90% of Canadians live within 160 km of the US border
  • The Main Street Corridor (Windsor–Quebec City) contains the densest concentration of population
  • Ecumene — the inhabited area of Canada — covers only a fraction of the total land
  • Urban population: over 80% of Canadians live in cities (one of the highest rates in the world)
  • Census Metropolitan Areas (CMAs) with 100,000+ population anchor regional economies

Immigration and Cultural Geography

  • Canada is one of the world's most immigration-dependent economies, accepting ~400,000+ new permanent residents annually
  • Top source countries in recent years: India, Philippines, China, Nigeria, France
  • Canada is officially multicultural — policy since 1971 under Trudeau (Pierre Elliott)
  • Bilingualism: English and French are official languages (Constitution Act 1982, Charter of Rights and Freedoms)
  • Francophone communities outside Quebec: Acadians (NB, NS, PEI, NL), Franco-Ontarians, Franco-Manitobans
  • Canada has one of the world's highest per-capita rates of immigration
Canadian History — Geographic Context

Key Dates and Events

~1,000 CE Norse explorer Leif Eriksson reaches Vinland (L'Anse aux Meadows, NL) — first confirmed European contact
1497 John Cabot (Giovanni Caboto) reaches Newfoundland for England — claims northeastern North America
1534–1542 Jacques Cartier maps the Gulf of St. Lawrence and names Canada (from Huron-Iroquois "kanata" meaning village/settlement)
1608 Samuel de Champlain founds Quebec City — first permanent French settlement
1670 Hudson's Bay Company (HBC) chartered — controls Rupert's Land (Hudson Bay watershed); major fur trade era begins
1763 Treaty of Paris — France cedes New France to Britain; Quebec Act 1774 protects French civil law and Catholic religion
1812–1815 War of 1812 — Canadian militia repels US invasion; burning of York (Toronto); Laura Secord's famous ride
1867 Confederation — British North America Act joins Ontario, Quebec, Nova Scotia, New Brunswick into the Dominion of Canada. Sir John A. Macdonald first PM.
1869–70 Red River Resistance — Louis Riel leads Métis; Manitoba becomes a province
1885 Canadian Pacific Railway completed — transcontinental link; Northwest Rebellion; Riel hanged
1896–1899 Klondike Gold Rush — Yukon; 100,000 prospectors; Dawson City boomtown
1999 Nunavut created — largest land claim settlement in Canadian history; Inuit-majority territory

Notable Canadian Explorers

  • Alexander Mackenzie — first European to cross North America overland (1793); Mackenzie River named after him
  • Samuel Hearne — first European to reach the Arctic Ocean overland (via Coppermine River, 1771)
  • David Thompson — mapped more of North America than any other person; charted the Columbia River system
  • Sir John Franklin — Arctic explorer whose 1845 expedition to find the Northwest Passage ended in disaster; all 129 crew died; HMS Erebus and Terror found 2014–2016
  • Matthew Flinders — mapped Australia (not Canada, common confusion)
  • Henry Hudson — Dutch-born, English-employed; explored Hudson Bay (1610–11); crew mutinied and left him there

Natural Resources — Energy, Mining & Agriculture

Oil & Natural Gas

  • Alberta's oil sands (Athabasca) — world's 3rd-largest proven oil reserves
  • Controversial: significant greenhouse gas emissions, tailings ponds, land disturbance
  • Trans Mountain Pipeline — carries oil from Alberta to BC coast; expanded 2024
  • Offshore: Hibernia platform (Grand Banks, NL); Sable Island (NS)
  • Fracking controversy in BC and Quebec

Hydroelectric Power

  • Canada is the 3rd-largest hydroelectric producer in the world
  • Quebec's James Bay Project (La Grande complex) — one of the largest hydro systems on Earth
  • BC: Columbia River, Peace River dams (Site C — controversial)
  • Manitoba: Churchill River system
  • Labrador: Churchill Falls (exports power to Quebec under controversial contract)
  • Hydro provides ~60% of Canada's electricity

Mining & Minerals

  • Canadian Shield: nickel (Sudbury — "Nickel Capital of the World"), gold, uranium, copper
  • NWT: diamonds (Canada is among the world's top producers of gem-quality diamonds)
  • Saskatchewan: potash — Canada produces ~30% of the world's potash (key fertilizer)
  • BC: copper, molybdenum, coal
  • Yukon/NWT: gold, silver, zinc, lead
  • Lithium increasingly sought for EV batteries

Forestry

  • Canada has ~9% of the world's forests; boreal forest is the world's largest intact forest
  • BC: softwoods (Douglas-fir, cedar) — major lumber exporter
  • Pulp and paper: Quebec and Ontario
  • Ongoing controversy: old-growth logging in BC (Fairy Creek blockade, 2021)
  • Sustainable Forestry Initiative (SFI) and FSC certification systems used

Agriculture

  • Saskatchewan and Manitoba: wheat, canola, barley, flaxseed
  • Ontario: corn, soybeans, vegetables, tender fruits (Niagara)
  • BC: Okanagan apples, wine grapes, berries
  • Alberta: cattle (beef production); feedlots
  • PEI: potatoes (seed and table)
  • Canada is among the world's top food exporters

Fisheries

  • Atlantic: cod (collapsed 1992 — Canadian Grand Banks cod moratorium)
  • Lobster, snow crab: major Atlantic industries; NS, NB, PEI, NL, QC
  • Pacific: Pacific salmon (5 species); BC; threatened by habitat loss, climate change
  • Great Lakes: commercial and sport fishery; invasive species (sea lamprey, zebra mussels) are major threats
  • Aquaculture growing — salmon farming in BC, mussels and oysters in Atlantic

Recent Canadian Geographic Magazine Stories — 2024–2025

January/February 2025

2025 Issues

  • "Born to Run" (2025) — Pronghorn antelope on the Canadian prairies, historically blocked by fencing; conservation volunteers reconnecting fragmented grassland corridors for North America's fastest land mammal.

Major Environmental Issues (2024–2026)

  • 2023 Wildfire Season — Record-breaking; over 18 million hectares burned across Canada; smoke blanketed US cities; widespread evacuation of northern communities including Yellowknife
  • Trans Mountain Pipeline Expansion — Completed 2024; tripled capacity; major oil-to-tanker debate; BC First Nations opposition
  • Gulf of St. Lawrence North Atlantic Right Whale Protection — Ongoing battle to protect critically endangered whales (~340 remaining) from fishing gear and shipping in Canadian waters
  • Great Bear Rainforest — Ongoing conservation challenges; old-growth protection; Spirit Bear populations
  • Northwest Passage Sovereignty — As Arctic ice melts, debate over whether the NWP is international waters (US/EU position) or Canadian internal waters (Canada's position)
  • UNDRIP — Canada adopted the UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples into federal law (Bill C-15, 2021); implementation ongoing

Global Geography — Canadian Geographic Perspective
At nationals: Questions test "global geographic thinking." Canada's connections to world systems — trade, climate agreements, biodiversity, international aid — are fair game alongside place-name identification.

Continents & Major Facts

  • Largest continent: Asia (44.6M km²)
  • Smallest continent: Australia/Oceania
  • Most populous: Asia (~4.7 billion)
  • Highest point: Mount Everest, Nepal/China (8,849 m)
  • Lowest land: Dead Sea shore (-430 m)
  • Largest ocean: Pacific (larger than all land combined)
  • Deepest ocean trench: Mariana Trench (11,000+ m)

World Rivers & Lakes

  • Longest river: Nile (Africa) or Amazon (contested by length vs. flow)
  • Largest river by discharge: Amazon
  • Largest lake: Caspian Sea (saltwater, technically a lake)
  • Largest freshwater lake: Lake Superior (shared Canada/USA)
  • Deepest lake: Lake Baikal, Russia (1,642 m)
  • Largest tropical rainforest: Amazon Basin

Biomes — Global

  • Largest biome: Taiga/boreal forest (circumpolar)
  • Most biodiverse biome: tropical rainforest
  • Largest desert: Antarctica (cold desert); largest hot desert: Sahara
  • Coral reefs: Great Barrier Reef (Australia) largest; severely bleached by warming oceans
  • Mangroves: critical coastal ecosystems, increasingly threatened

Global Environmental Issues

  • Paris Agreement: target to limit warming to 1.5°C above pre-industrial levels
  • COP summits: annual UN climate conferences
  • Biodiversity: UN target — 30×30 (protect 30% of land and ocean by 2030)
  • Deforestation: Amazon and Congo Basin under pressure
  • Plastic pollution: Pacific and Atlantic garbage patches
  • Ocean acidification: absorbs 30% of CO₂ emissions

Fieldwork Exercise — How to Prepare
What happened at the 2025 final: Finalists went outdoors in Ottawa for hands-on fieldwork — observing, mapping, and analyzing geographic features in the real world. Think like a geographer, not just a trivia player.
  • Map Reading & Topographic Maps Practice reading topographic maps (contour lines, scale, legend, compass rose, grid references). Know how to calculate slope, identify landforms from contours, and find elevations. Natural Resources Canada topographic maps are the Canadian standard.
  • Compass Skills & Orienteering Know how to use a compass to determine direction and bearing. Practice taking bearings from landmarks. Understand magnetic declination in Canada (varies by region). Be able to navigate a route using map + compass.
  • Environmental Observation Practice identifying rock types (igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic), soil horizons, vegetation communities, drainage patterns, and signs of human modification of the landscape. What is the evidence of glaciation? What is the land use in this area?
  • Sketch Mapping Practice drawing quick, accurate sketch maps of an area with: title, scale (bar scale), north arrow, legend/key, labels for key features. Must be clear enough for someone unfamiliar with the area to understand.
  • Land Use Analysis Identify and categorize land uses visible in an area (residential, commercial, industrial, agricultural, conservation, institutional). Be able to explain why certain land uses appear where they do geographically.
  • Water Features Analysis Understand watershed, drainage basins, floodplains, river meanders, erosion and deposition. Identify how a river changes as it moves from upper to lower course. Look for evidence of human modification (dams, channelization, berms).
  • Geographic Questions Framework At any fieldwork site, ask: What is here? Why is it here? How did it form? How does it connect to the broader landscape? What are the human impacts? What will it look like in the future? These six questions mirror how geographers think.
Practice tip: Go outside at least once a week before the final. Walk a local area — a park, a ravine, a waterfront — and practise observing, sketching, and describing it geographically. Ottawa is rich in geography: the Ottawa River, Rideau Canal, Gatineau Hills, Greenbelt, glacial features, and urban land use contrasts.

Live Trivia Final — Strategy and Format

Speed + Accuracy

The game-show format rewards both correctness and speed. Wrong answers typically lose points. When unsure, your best-reasoned guess is often worth attempting. Don't freeze — commit.

Elimination Reasoning

Even in buzzer rounds, think: "Which options can I eliminate immediately?" Narrow to your best choice. Canada's geography is systematic — use what you know about regions to narrow possibilities.

Bilingual Confidence

You may compete in English or French. If a question involves French place names (Gaspésie, Charlevoix, Nunavik), you're expected to know them. French geographic vocabulary helps even in English play.

Watch Past Finals

Past national final videos are on Can Geo Education's YouTube channel. Watching even 2–3 episodes teaches you the exact pacing, question style, and difficulty level better than any guide can.

Province/territory capitals (all 13) — know them perfectly

Practice Questions — Test Yourself
Physical Geography Practice
Q1: What is the largest physiographic region of Canada?
A: The Canadian Shield — covers roughly half of Canada's land area, centred on Hudson Bay.
Q2: Which river is the longest entirely within Canada?
A: The Mackenzie River system (4,241 km total length), draining into the Arctic Ocean from Great Slave Lake in NWT.
Q3: What is Canada's highest mountain, and where is it located?
A: Mount Logan (5,959 m) in the St. Elias Mountains, Yukon. It is the highest peak in Canada and the second-highest in North America.
Q4: Name the Great Lake with the highest recorded tidal range nearby.
A: The Great Lakes themselves have no significant tides. The world's highest tides are in the Bay of Fundy, Nova Scotia/New Brunswick — not associated with the Great Lakes.
Q5: What geological process created most of Canada's lake basins?
A: Glaciation — glaciers during the last Ice Age scoured basins and deposited moraines that dammed valleys, creating lakes when meltwater filled them.
Q6: Which province contains the Athabasca Oil Sands, and why are they controversial?
A: Alberta. They are controversial because extraction generates significant greenhouse gas emissions, creates massive tailings ponds, disturbs vast areas of boreal forest, and impacts Indigenous communities and traditional territories.
Human Geography & Current Events Practice
Q7: Which is Canada's newest territory, when was it created, and who are the majority of its residents?
A: Nunavut, created April 1, 1999, from the eastern part of the Northwest Territories. The majority of residents are Inuit.
Q8: What are Inuit Nunangat's four regions?
A: Nunatsiavut (northern Labrador), Nunavik (northern Quebec), Nunavut (territory), and the Inuvialuit Settlement Region (NWT/Yukon Arctic coast).
Q9: The Grand Banks cod moratorium occurred in what year, and what caused the collapse?
A: 1992. Decades of over-fishing depleted the cod stocks beyond recovery; modern trawling technology allowed far greater catches than were sustainable.
Q10: Canada has the world's longest coastline. Approximately how long is it?
A: Approximately 202,080 km — the longest coastline of any country in the world.
Q11: What is the Klondike Gold Rush, and in which decade did it occur?
A: A gold rush that brought approximately 100,000 prospectors to the Yukon (primarily around Dawson City and Bonanza Creek) between 1896 and 1899.
Q12: What does "Reconciliation" mean in the context of Canadian policy and the Truth and Reconciliation Commission?
A: Reconciliation refers to the process of repairing the relationship between Indigenous and non-Indigenous Canadians, addressing the harms caused by residential schools, colonialism, and related policies. The TRC's 94 Calls to Action (2015) provide a roadmap for this work.
Biomes, Wildlife & Parks Practice
Q13: What is the Spirit Bear, and where is it found?
A: The Spirit Bear (Kermode bear) is a rare white-furred subspecies of the North American black bear. It is found only in the Great Bear Rainforest of BC's central and north coast, and is sacred to many coastal First Nations.
Q14: Which national park contains the largest free-roaming herd of wild bison in North America?
A: Wood Buffalo National Park (on the Alberta/NWT border). It is also the largest national park in Canada and a UNESCO World Heritage Site.
Q15: What is permafrost, and why does its thaw matter?
A: Permafrost is ground that remains frozen for two or more consecutive years. Its thaw due to climate change releases stored carbon (methane), destabilizes Arctic infrastructure (buildings, roads, runways), and alters drainage patterns. It accelerates climate change in a positive feedback loop.
Q16: Name Canada's two most endangered large marine mammals.
A: The North Atlantic right whale (approximately 340 remaining, threatened in the Gulf of St. Lawrence) and the Beluga whale (St. Lawrence population is small and endangered).
